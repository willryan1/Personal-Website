Like privately owned corporations, foreign state-owned entities often become entangled in litigation in United States courts. The Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act (FSIA) established certain limits on the extent of such litigation by granting foreign states the presumption of immunity from suit in U.S. courts. The FSIA's protection extends somewhat broadly, including within its coverage foreign states proper, as well as the "agencies or instrumentalities" of foreign states. Determining precisely what constitutes an agency or instrumentality, however, is a difficult process. As U.S. plaintiffs seek to sue foreign stateowned corporations, one vexing yet unresolved question is becoming increasingly important and contentious: whether a corporation owned by an agency or instrumentality of a foreign state is itself part of the foreign state for purposes of the FSIA.

This question centers on so-called "tiered corporations," those with multiple layers of corporate ownership, in which intermediate corporations buffer a corporate entity from its ultimate owner-in this context, a foreign state. While the contours of tiered corporations vary, a typical corporate structure is illustrated in Gardiner Stone Hunter Int'l. v. Iberia Lineas Aereas de Espafia, S.A. Gardiner centers on the activities of Aerolineas Argentinas, an Argentina based corporation owned by Iberia Lineas Aereas de Espafia, S.A., a corporation owned by the Spanish government. Aerolineas' ownership structure is said to be "tiered," since Spain's ownership of Aerolineas Argentinas is indirect, i.e. mediated through an intermediate corporation in the form of Iberia Lineas Aereas de Espafia.
